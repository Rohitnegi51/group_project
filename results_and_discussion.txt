# 4. Results and Discussion

## 4.1. Experimental Setup and Evaluation Strategy
The primary objective of this study was to rigorously evaluate the efficacy of three distinct metaheuristic feature selection algorithms—the Chaotic Reptile Search Algorithm (CRSA), Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO), and a hybridized Self-Organizing Map coupled with a Genetic Algorithm (SOM-GA). These algorithms were employed to optimize the input feature space for a Random Forest Classifier (RFC) tasked with detecting and diagnosing faults in Photovoltaic (PV) arrays. 

The evaluation was conducted utilizing a comprehensive photovoltaic dataset that captured both fundamental environmental parameters (e.g., Solar Irradiance, Module Temperature) and electrical characteristics (e.g., Maximum Power Point Voltage $V_{max}$, Maximum Power Point Current $I_{max}$, Open-Circuit Voltage $V_{oc}$, and Short-Circuit Current $I_{sc}$). To enrich the feature space and provide the algorithms with non-linear relationships, 15 synthetic features were derived during the feature engineering phase, including critical electrical ratios (e.g., $V_{oc}/V_{max}$, $I_{sc}/I_{max}$) and deltas (e.g., $I_{sc} - I_{max}$). 

The classification targets were strictly categorized into three distinct operational states: Partial Shading (PS), String Mismatch (MM), and Normal (Healthy) operations. To ensure a robust assessment of the selected feature subsets, the Random Forest Classifier was evaluated using a standardized suite of performance metrics: Accuracy, Precision, Recall, and the F1-Score, derived from a stratified train-test evaluation framework.

## 4.2. Quantitative Performance Efficacy
The empirical results, as summarized in Table I, demonstrate that all three wrapper-based feature selection methods successfully navigated the high-dimensional feature space, extracting subsets that enabled the Random Forest Classifier to achieve perfect classification accuracy (100%) on the evaluated validation set.

In stark contrast, CRSA required 6 features to achieve the same capability, while PSO required 6 features and still failed catastrophically.

## 4.3. Algorithmic Behavioral Analysis

### 4.3.1. Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO)
PSO, driven by the collective social and cognitive intelligence of flying particles, demonstrated the poorest performance in this study. It retained 6 features but plummeted to a 66.7% accuracy, completely failing to identify Partial Shading faults. While PSO is renowned for rapid convergence, its binary variant often suffers from premature convergence when navigating discrete feature spaces. Because the fitness function heavily prioritized early classification accuracy, the PSO particles rapidly converged upon a local optimum that could identify severe Mismatch faults but lacked the nuance to detect Shading. Once this local optimum was reached, the velocity vectors driving the particles decayed, trapping the swarm.

### 4.3.2. Chaotic Reptile Search Algorithm (CRSA)
CRSA showed a marginal improvement over PSO, terminating with a 7-feature subset (`Irradiance`, `Resistance`, `p_ratio`, `voc_isc_ratio`, `pmax_isc_ratio`, `vmax_imax_ratio`, `isc_minus_imax`). The integration of the Chaotic Logistic Map for population initialization allowed CRSA to explore the initial feature space more uniformly than PSO. Furthermore, the alternating exploration (high walking) and exploitation (hunting) mechanics of the reptile search heuristic allowed it to discard one additional redundant thermal feature (`Temp`) that PSO failed to eliminate. However, similar to PSO, CRSA's reliance on a strictly supervised fitness score prevented it from penalizing intra-feature correlations, leaving residual redundancy in the electrical ratio features.

### 4.3.3. Self-Organizing Maps + Genetic Algorithm (SOM-GA)
The overwhelming superiority of the SOM-GA approach stems directly from its hybridized, semi-supervised fitness evaluation mechanism. Unlike PSO and CRSA, which relied solely on the downstream K-Nearest Neighbor (KNN) accuracy to guide their search, the SOM-GA fitness function integrated the **Quantization Error** of an unsupervised Self-Organizing Map. 

By simultaneously maximizing classification accuracy and minimizing the SOM quantization error, the Genetic Algorithm was forced to optimize for both predictive power *and* topological cluster separability. The SOM component acts as an inherent penalty against redundant or noisy features; features that do not contribute to distinct, tightly knit topological clusters on the SOM grid proportionately increase the quantization error, thereby lowering the chromosome's fitness. 

This dual-pressure environment allowed the evolutionary operators (crossover and mutation) of the GA to systematically strip away highly correlated features, distilling the dataset down to its 4 most critical components: `Irradiance`, `p_ratio`, `isc_minus_imax`, and `imax_isc_ratio`.

## 4.4. Physical Significance of the SOM-GA Feature Subset
The parsimony achieved by SOM-GA is not merely a mathematical artifact; the specific 4 features selected hold profound physical significance in the domain of PV fault diagnostics:
1. **`Irradiance`**: The fundamental environmental baseline that dictates the theoretical maximum power output of the array. Without this context, normal low-light operations cannot be distinguished from shading faults.
2. **`p_ratio` ($P_{max} / (V_{max} \times I_{max})$)**: This Fill Factor analog is a direct indicator of the PV curve's degradation. Severe deviation in this ratio is a classic hallmark of Partial Shading conditions where multiple power peaks distort the standard IV curve.
3. **`isc_minus_imax` ($I_{sc} - I_{max}$)**: This delta is critical for identifying String Mismatches. Under mismatch conditions, bypass diodes activate, causing a disproportionate shift between the short-circuit current and the current at the maximum power point.
4. **`imax_isc_ratio` ($I_{max} / I_{sc}$)**: Acting synergistically with the delta feature, this normalized ratio ensures that current mismatches can be detected uniformly across varying irradiance levels, preventing false positives during fluctuating weather conditions.

The fact that SOM-GA autonomously identified this physically comprehensive yet mathematically minimal subset highlights its exceptional capability for feature extraction in power systems engineering.

## 4.5. Implications for Real-World Deployment and Future Work
The reduction of the required feature space from over 15 variables down to 4 has massive implications for the deployment of intelligent PV monitoring systems. 

First, it drastically reduces the hardware requirements. Sensing networks no longer need to rely on highly calibrated temperature sensors or complex resistance estimators if the diagnostic algorithm can rely entirely on standard electrical metering ($I, V, P$) and a single irradiance pyranometer. Second, reducing the input vector to $N=4$ exponentially decreases the computational complexity $O(n \log n)$ of training and running inference through the Random Forest Classifier on embedded microcontrollers or IoT edge devices.

While the current results are highly promising, achieving 100% accuracy highlights a limitation of the current dataset's complexity. Future work will involve expanding the empirical validation to larger, noisier datasets encompassing a wider array of fault typologies (e.g., bypass diode failures, line-to-line faults, and degradation over time). It is hypothesized that as dataset complexity and noise increase, the topological clustering constraints of the SOM-GA will provide even greater performance separation from traditional algorithms like PSO and CRSA, preventing overfitting while maintaining high diagnostic precision.
