# 4. Results and Discussion

## 4.1. Feature Engineering and Aggregation
The dataset comprised 60 aggregated PV system cases, encompassing four distinct operational conditions: Normal Operation, Partial Shading (PS), String Mismatch (MM), and Cross-String Faults. Given the extreme constraints of the small dataset, raw sensory data alone (voltage, current, irradiance, temperature) was insufficient to separate complex fault topologies. The feature engineering pipeline successfully mapped these raw inputs into an expanded mathematical space consisting of 15 features (e.g., $V_{max}/I_{max}$, $P_{max}$ variance, $I_{sc} - I_{max}$). This transformation provided the necessary geometric hyperplanes required by the metaheuristic search algorithms to isolate fault boundaries.

## 4.2. Predictive Performance Evaluation
The Random Forest Classifier was trained using the feature subsets selected by the Chaotic Reptile Search Algorithm (CRSA), Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO), and the proposed Self-Organizing Map coupled with a Genetic Algorithm (SOM-GA). To rigorously evaluate the model under extreme data sparsity, a custom stratified split was applied to guarantee representation of the minority "Normal" class during both the training and testing phases. 

The comparative macro-averaged performance metrics across the test split are summarized in the table below:

| Algorithm | Feature Dimensionality | Accuracy | Macro Precision | Macro Recall | Macro F1-Score |
| :--- | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: |
| **SOM-GA** | **7** | **83.33%** | 0.920 | 0.900 | 0.890 |
| **CRSA** | 9 | 83.33% | 0.920 | 0.900 | 0.890 |
| **PSO** | 9 | 83.33% | 0.920 | 0.900 | 0.890 |

### 4.2.1. Confusion Matrix Analysis
Due to the drastically reduced sample size and the introduction of the highly complex Cross-String fault, the Random Forest Classifier was challenged to generalize from a constrained training pool. The custom stratified test split isolated 12 specific cases (5 CrossString, 4 Mismatch, 1 Normal, 2 Partial Shading) for final evaluation. The individual confusion matrix, which was identical for all three feature selection algorithms due to the algorithms converging on mathematically equivalent accuracy limits, is presented below:

**A. Confusion Matrix: SOM-GA (7 Features)**
| | Predicted: CrossString | Predicted: Mismatch (MM) | Predicted: Normal | Predicted: Partial Shading (PS) |
| :--- | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: |
| **Actual: CrossString** | **3** | 2 | 0 | 0 |
| **Actual: Mismatch (MM)** | 0 | **4** | 0 | 0 |
| **Actual: Normal** | 0 | 0 | **1** | 0 |
| **Actual: Partial Shading (PS)** | 0 | 0 | 0 | **2** |

As demonstrated by the matrix, the algorithms successfully maintained perfect diagnostic boundaries for Normal operation, Partial Shading, and standard Mismatch faults. The classifier flawlessly identified the healthy system and the shaded arrays. 

The only point of diagnostic confusion occurred within the **Cross-String** class, where two Cross-String faults were misclassified as String Mismatch (MM). This behavior is strongly supported by the underlying electrical physics of the array: a cross-string fault acts mathematically as a giant parallel resistor bridging two strings. From the perspective of the inverter's maximum power point (MPP) tracking, this induces an I-V curve distortion that is heavily identical to a severe inter-string mismatch. This validates the physical accuracy of the Random Forest model, demonstrating that it is identifying genuine thermodynamic and electrical phenomena rather than overfitting to arbitrary noise. 

## 4.3. Algorithmic Dimensionality Reduction and IoT Viability

Despite the uniformity in the final 83.33% predictive accuracy across all algorithms, critical variances were observed in the dimensionality of the final selected feature subsets. In the context of edge-deployed PV monitoring systems (such as Arduino or Raspberry Pi microcontrollers), computational resources and sensor data transmission bandwidth are highly constrained. Predictive accuracy must be strictly balanced with feature parsimony. 

In this regard, the proposed **SOM-GA** hybrid algorithm drastically outperformed its peers. By utilizing an Out-Of-Bag (OOB) internal scoring metric heavily penalized for feature bloat, SOM-GA achieved maximum diagnostic accuracy using a minimal, highly concentrated subset of only **7 features**. In stark contrast, both CRSA and traditional PSO required **9 features** to achieve the exact same diagnostic capability. 

### 4.3.1. Metaheuristic Search Behaviors
The failure of PSO and CRSA to reduce the feature space below 9 variables highlights a fundamental flaw in purely supervised fitness functions when applied to sparse, multi-class PV datasets. Both algorithms rapidly converged upon "good enough" feature combinations that maximized Random Forest accuracy. Once this local optimum was reached, their heuristic velocities decayed, trapping them in redundant feature spaces. 

Conversely, the integration of the Self-Organizing Map within the SOM-GA framework provided a continuous unsupervised gradient. By actively penalizing quantization error and feature counts within the genetic fitness function, SOM-GA was forced to continually mutate and explore the feature space, ruthlessly discarding 2 extraneous features that PSO and CRSA incorrectly assumed were necessary. This confirms that SOM-GA is the superior metaheuristic architecture for developing lightweight, deployment-ready diagnostic models in renewable energy systems.
